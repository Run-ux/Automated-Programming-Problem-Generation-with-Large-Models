A. Arranging Teams

You are the coach of a competitive programming team with $n$ members. After months of intense training, your team is ready for the "Annual International Coding Pageant" (AICP). In this tournament, teams compete against each other in a series of one-on-one matches.

The tournament structure is peculiar. There are $m$ other teams, and your team will play against each of them. For every match against an opponent team, the players are lined up, and the $i$ -th player from your team competes against the $i$ -th player from the opponent's team.

Each of your team members has a known strength value. The opponent teams have already submitted their player arrangements, and these are fixed. Your task is to find a single, optimal arrangement for your own team members that will be used against all m opponent teams to maximize your total score across the entire tournament.

You have a team of $n$ members with strengths given by an array of size $n$. You are competing against $m$ other teams. The $k$ -th opponent team (for $k$ from $1$ to $m$) also has $n$ members, with strengths given by $b_{k,1}, b_{k,2},..., b_{k,n}$.

You need to find a permutation $^{\text{∗}}$ $p$ of length $n$. This permutation $p$ will be your team's final arrangement for all matches. When your team (arranged as $p$) competes against opponent team $k$, your player at position $i$ (with strength $a_{p_i}$) is matched against their player at position $i$ (with strength $b_{k,i}$).

Your team's total score is the sum of points from all individual competitions. That is, the sum of scores for each of your $n$ players against their counterparts in all $m$ opponent teams.

Your goal is to find the arrangement $p$ that maximizes this total score.

$^{\text{∗}}$ A permutation of length $n$ is an array consisting of $n$ distinct integers from $1$ to $n$ in arbitrary order. For example, $[2,3,1,5,4]$ is a permutation, but $[1,2,2]$ is not a permutation ($2$ appears twice in the array), and $[1,3,4]$ is also not a permutation ($n=3$ but there is $4$ in the array).

Input
The first line contains two integers, $n$ ($1 \le n \le 20$) and $m$ ($1 \le m \le 5\,000$), the number of members per team and the number of opponent teams. The second line contains n space-separated integers $a_1, a_2,..., a_n$, representing the strengths of your team members ($1 \le a_i \le 10^9$). The next $m$ lines each contain n space-separated integers. The $k$ -th of these lines represents the strengths of the $k$ -th opponent team: $b_{k,1}, b_{k,2},..., b_{k,n}$ ($1 \le b_{k,i} \le 10^9$).

Output
Print a single line containing $n$ space-separated integers, representing the optimal arrangement of your team's strengths that maximizes the total score. If multiple optimal arrangements exist, any one of them will be accepted. Formally, let $p$ be the optimal permutation, you must output $a_{p_1},a_{p_2},\dots,a_{p_n}$ separated by whitespace in a single line.

Constraints
time limit per test 1 second
memory limit per test 256 megabytes
